Greenroom Design canvas Slice 04 · Agent sign-off v0.1

The page where
Daniel turns a draft
into signed.

Daniel-at-WME taps the magic link between meetings, on his phone, in a hallway. He has thirty seconds to confirm the deal Mariana sent, sign, and move on. Four different design philosophies applied to the same anatomy — phone-native quick-sign, full-document ceremony, split review-and-sign, and a conversational letter that reads like a reply to Mariana’s thread.

00 / 05

The anatomy

Eight pieces from the productspec. Every variation includes all eight; they differ in how they’re arranged, weighted, and framed.
01
Header

Show name + date

Branded prompt: “Please review and sign the deal below.” Subhead names the artist and venue.

02
Recap

Structured deal

Every term rendered explicitly — including bucket and applies-at on each recoup, and the full deduction order.

03
Source

Original email

Collapsed by default. Visible if the agent wants to compare against the original message that started the negotiation.

04
Signature

Draw + Type tabs

Draw canvas (touch on phone). Type field renders the typed name in a script preview. Small "clear" link.

05
Printed name

Required text field

A second proof of identity alongside the captured signature. Auto-fills from the recipient on the magic link, but editable.

06
Agreement

"I agree" checkbox

Single sentence: this constitutes my electronic signature and I have authority to bind the artist to these terms. Required.

07
Sign / Decline

Two paths

Sign and confirm — gated on signature + name + checkbox. Decline with comment — lighter, no signature.

08
Escape hatch

Reply to Mariana

Small link near the bottom: “Need to talk? Reply to the original email.” A safety valve for “I don’t want to commit either way yet.”

The four variations differ in framing, not in field set. Every one of the eight pieces above is present in all four designs. What changes is how the agent feels on arrival — in a rush, in a ceremony, with the recap always in view, or like they’re replying to a friend.

A.3

Why this shape

Notes on the design choices that make this variation phone-native.
  • Thumb zone
    Sign button bottom-aligned within natural thumb reach. The signature canvas sits above it — you draw then immediately sign without scrolling.
  • Draw default
    Draw tab is selected by default on touch devices. Type falls back to a script-style preview for desktop / fingers not behaving.
  • Source collapsed
    Original email is one tap away, not in your face. Most agents trust the structured recap they sent the terms from.
  • Decline isn’t scary
    Decline link is a single line below Sign. Lighter weight, but always visible. No signature required to decline.
  • Reply escape hatch
    "Reply to the original email" for the agents who want to negotiate inline. Always reachable.
Why this works. Matches the dominant reality — agents read on phones, between meetings. The vertical scroll fits the medium; the touch-canvas signature is the input agents most want when signing from a phone. Why it might not. Less ceremony might feel too casual for higher-value deals. The agent might sign without really reading. Pair with mandatory scroll-to-bottom or a small "you’ve seen the recoups" inline marker for the deals where it matters.

What changes on the phone
  • 01 Toolbar collapses to a slim band — progress dots + "3 / 5" only; the secondary actions (Original email, Help) move into a ⋮ menu.
  • 02 Section padding tightens, fonts step down one notch, but the green-bordered "Reviewed" marker stays — it’s the soul of the ceremony.
  • 03 Signature panel goes single-column: Draw canvas → printed name → checkbox → action buttons. No more side-by-side name/date.
  • 04 Sign / Decline stack vertically instead of side-by-side. Sign is the primary thumb target; Decline sits directly below as an outline button (still equal-weight in semantics, just space-efficient).
  • 05 Original email collapses into a tappable section row instead of an expandable card — one less thing to scroll past.
Why this works. The progress toolbar plus section-by-section “reviewed” markers give the agent a sense of doing-due-diligence without forcing it. Decline and Sign sit side-by-side — equal weight, no hidden friction. Why it might not. Heavy. Daniel-on-phone-between-meetings will scroll past most of it. Works better when the deal is high-stakes or when the artist’s legal team is going to look at it anyway.

What changes on the phone
  • 01 Two columns become two tabs — Review and Sign — with the recap section count and a ✓ indicator. The split layout’s "always-visible" promise becomes "always-one-tap-away".
  • 02 A compact recap card at the top of the Sign tab keeps the headline numbers in view while signing. The full recap stays one tap away under Review.
  • 03 "← Back to full review" link right above the signature panel — the equivalent of glancing left on desktop.
  • 04 Sign panel takes the rest of the screen. Single-column Draw / Type, name, checkbox, big thumb-zone Sign button. Decline is a lighter link below.
  • 05 The Review tab (not shown) is functionally identical to the variation A scroll — recap blocks stacked, no signature panel. A floating "Sign now ✓" pill anchors back to this tab.
Why this works. The always-visible recap means the agent can verify anything mid-signing without losing the sign panel. Sticky behavior makes the long-scroll recap feel safe. Why it might not. On phones the layout’s "two columns" advantage turns into a tab toggle, which is functionally similar to variation A’s pure scroll. The choice between A and C is essentially: do you want an explicit Review-vs-Sign mental model, or a single scroll?

What changes on the phone
  • 01 Wrapped in a Mail-app shell — back arrow, thread name, kebab menu. The page becomes a thread instead of resembling one.
  • 02 Sender row uses the standard email pattern: avatar, name, to-line. Daniel reads it the same way he reads every other email from Mariana.
  • 03 Letter typography shrinks by one step; inline deal-term cards stay full-width and stack naturally between paragraphs.
  • 04 Sign panel reads as the reply composer. Tabs say Sign & agree / Reply with note — same actions, channel-native vocabulary.
  • 05 Final CTA is "Send signed reply", not "Sign and confirm". The framing keeps a soft, relational tone all the way through the signing moment.
Why this works. Lowers the “contract” signal — for relationships where the deal is more handshake-than-paperwork, this format feels native to the channel. The "send signed reply" button frames the action as responding to a message, not signing a document. Why it might not. Soft framing might undersell the legal weight. Agents at WME, CAA, etc. who do this for a living might prefer the document ceremony of variation B. Best for indie / repeat agent relationships.
05 / 05

Compare

Four philosophies, four hypotheses about what makes an agent comfortable signing.
A · Phone-first
Vertical scroll, thumb-zone sign.
Best for the 80% case — agents reading on phones between meetings.

Risk: too casual for high-stakes deals.

Recommended default
B · Document
Section markers + progress.
Best for high-value deals or agency lawyers who want due diligence on record.

Risk: friction. Agents will skim past most of it on phone.
C · Split
Recap left, sign sticky right.
Best for desktop signing — GM-on-couch, agent on laptop.

Risk: collapses to A on phones, so the layout edge only matters on bigger screens.
D · Letter
Reply to Mariana, with terms inline.
Best for repeat indie agent relationships where the deal feels like a handshake.

Risk: can undersell legal weight. Doesn’t suit majors.
Recommendation
Ship A · Phone-first as the default agent sign-off page — it matches the dominant channel (phone) and the dominant mood (between meetings). Borrow B’s “reviewed” section markers as an optional progressive-disclosure enhancement when a deal is flagged high-stakes or when the agency configures it. Keep C’s split layout as the desktop upgrade — the same page reflows into two columns on screens > 1024px, so Marcus on his couch gets the always-visible recap automatically. Skip D for v1 — the letter format is a beautiful idea, but agents at the agencies booking The Crescent (WME, CAA, UTA) likely want the sober document. Revisit if a venue gets a lot of indie / direct-from-artist deals.

The four variations share the universal anatomy from §00. Build the eight pieces as composable React components once; let the layout swap be a thin shell decision driven by viewport, recipient role, or venue preference.
